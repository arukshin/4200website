Introduction

This dataset compiles a list of asteroids that NASA has identified as Near-Earth Objects (NEOs), highlighting those that are closest to our planet and could potentially cause harm. In outer space, there are an infinite amount of objects, some of which are much closer to Earth than most people imagine. For example, even a distance as seemingly large as an asteroid being 70,000 kilometers is quite small when the astronomical scale is taken into account. As a result, harmful asteroids at this distance can pose a threat and disrupt many natural phenomena. This dataset allows viewers to gain insight into the proximity and characteristics of NEOs, increasing awareness of potential harmful objects.

For more information on the characteristics of near earth objects, read NEO Basics, which provides information on the basics of near Earth objects. Read the following paper to understand the dangers of hazardous Near Earth Objects.

Our Data and Preprocessing

This dataset contains descriptive information regarding objects in space near Earth. The CSV file contains 10 columns and 90,614 rows of data to work with. It contains important information for each asteroid that was monitored such as its name, approximate minimum size, approximate maximum size, distance it missed Earth by, relative velocity, magnitude, and if it was presumed dangerous or not. This dataset contains both categorical and numerical forms of data to work with and contains a minimal amount of missing data. This dataset was acquired from Kaggle. They pulled this data from the NASA Open API as well as NEO Earth Close Approaches. This dataset provides necessary information to perform an analysis on the potential dangers of objects near Earth. However, it only contains data regarding Earth so we cannot make any comparisons to other planets as well with this dataset.

To preprocess the data, we cut the size down to 2000 rows, as this makes visuallzing the features of the data much simpler. In addition, we created a 'year' column by extracting the year from the 'name' column. after this process was complete, we ultimately decided to drop the 'name' column as the name only contians the NEO ID and year of the NEO, both of which already exist as columns in our dataframe. After preprocessing was complete, our data included the following features:

Variable Name Description
id NEO ID
year Year the NEO was observed
est_diameter_min estimated minimum diameter of the NEO
est_diameter_max estimated maximum diameter of the NEO
miss_distance distance the NEO missed earth by
orbiting_body what planet the NEO orbits
sentry_object whether or not it was included in sentry (a collision monitoring system)
absolute_magnitude describes intrinisic luminosity
hazardous indicates whether or not the NEO was harmful

Average Miss distance Per Year

This figure visualizes the years with the highest and lowest average miss distances relative to Earth. The year 1960 has the highest average miss distance, suggesting that during this year, the NEOs passing by the Earth were generally further away than in other years. Conversely, 1950 had the lowest average miss distance, indicating that NEOs were, on average, significantly closer to Earth during this period. However, most years have a similar average miss distance, especially from 2004-2022. This can be used as a baseline in order to predict miss distance for future years, since the general trend of miss distances may remain stable over time.

Conclusion and Future Directions

Overall, our analysis provides valuable insights into the trends and characteristics of Near Earth Objects From a visual standpoint, hazardous NEOs tend to have a higher velocity relative to non-hazardous objects. While our remaining visualizations show some patterns related to hazardous objects, it is unclear if these patterns are significant or not.

To better understand how our features work together, we would like to incorporate a predictive model to see if features of hazardous objects are distinguishable from features of non-hazardous objects.
